Public Opinion Analysis

Experts in health policy have issued a warning about the potential health and economic consequences of growing obesity rates in the United States. Despite this, obesity has only lately gained political prominence in the United States, and little is known about what ordinary Americans think about obesity or whether they favor obesity-related legislation. Using unique survey data obtained by the authors, this research explores public attitudes of obesity and obesity policy. Contrary to common assumption, most Americans are indifferent about obesity, have little support for obesity-related efforts, and continue to assume that obesity is caused by individual failure rather than environmental or inherited causes. Given the paucity of elite discourse on obesity policy, we also show that typical drivers of policy preferences, such as ideology or partisanship, are not good predictors of attitudes on obesity policy. People instead employ other attitudinal frameworks, such as their opinions on smoking regulation and environmental accountability, when it comes to a low-valence problem like obesity. These findings are discussed in the context of obesity policy and public opinion research.
